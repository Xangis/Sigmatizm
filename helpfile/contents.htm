SigmaTizm
A Virtual Additive Synthesizer
Version 1.01

SigmaTizm is an additive synthesizer with 128 harmonic oscillators. For more about how additive synthesis works, you can read this intro.

It can be controlled using the keys of your computer keyboard, with each row of keys representing a different octave. A key will light up with a red circle when its note is playing. It can also be controlled with the mouse or via an external MIDI keyboard.

Harmonics Editor

The top left portion of the window is the harmonics editor. This is where you define the timbre of the sound the synthesizer will play. SigmaTizm supports 128 oscillators, with each bar representing a harmonic. The first bar represents the base frequency of the key being played, the second bar 2x that frequency, the third 3x that frequency, and so on.

Clicking in the harmonics editor will change the level for a particular harmonic and right-clicking will clear that harmonic. If notes are playing, you should hear the change in timbre. In addition, the changes should be reflected in either the 'attack' or 'sustain' waveform preview.

You can also left-click and drag to draw harmonics and right-click and drag to erase harmonics. Doing this while a note is playing will let you morph the sound in realtime for interesting filter effects.

The harmonics editor starts with a simple plain vanilla sine wave fundamental in both the attack and sustain phases of sound generation. You can change these two sets of harmonics independently by clicking the "Attack" button to edit attack harmonics and the "Sustain" button to edit sustain harmonics. If you want to copy from one panel to the other, the arrow buttons between the "Attack" and "Sustain" buttons will let you do so.

If you would like to start building with the harmonic series for a standard waveform (square, triangle, etc.) you can select it from the waveform list and click the "Generate" button. The panel will then contain the harmonic series for that waveform at levels appropriate for the "Target Polyphony" setting.

The "Target Polyphony" setting controls overall volume levels when generating or normalizing waveforms and is meant to determine the number of notes you can safely play without distortion. The "Safe Polyphony" number is an indicator of the number of notes that can be played simultaneously without "annoying" the soft limiter and is the maximum number of notes that can be played without the total peak level adding up to 1.0. Beyond that level, there is the danger of "clipping" which flattens the peaks of the output sound, whicn is often (but not always) undesirable.

Clcking the "Normalize" buttons will re-scale the harmonics in the editor window to more closely match the target polyphony level. This can be used to easily increase or decrease the overall volume of a sound you have created.

The "Clear" button removes all harmonics from the editor window.

The slider to the right of the window controls the "zoom level" of the harmonics window and lets you edit lower-level harmonics in greater detail. The number above the slider will show the maximum harmonic level for that zoom level.

Envelope Settings

The envelope settings let you control a standard ADSR (attack, decay, sustain, release) envelope for SigmaTizm. By default the attack time and decay time are both zero, which means notes will go into sustain phase as soon as they are played. If you want to hear both the attack and sustain portions of your harmonic series you will have to increase the attack and/or decay values.

The attack time is the amount of time it takes to go from zero to full volume. The decay time is the amount of time it takes to go from full volume to sustain volume. The sustain volume is a percent of max volume to sustain sounds at, and the release time is the amount of time it takes a sound to decay after the note stops playing.

Attack and decay settings have a maximum time of 1 second, while release has a maximum time of 1/2 second. All slider time values are in milliseconds.

When playing notes, the attack harmonic will start playing at zero volume. It will gradually increase in volume until the attack time is reached, then it will gradually decrease in volume toward the sustain level for the duration of the decay time. During this time, the harmonics that are playing will gradually transform from the attack harmonics to the sustain harmonics. If the attack and decay time are zero, then the harmonic transformation does not take place and the sustain harmonic plays immediately. If the sustain level is set at 100, then the volume level does not decrease during the decay phase. When a note is released, it will continue playing the sustain harmonics at lower volumes until the release time is reached.

Because harmonics switch to sustain harmonics when a note is released, there can be a pop or click sound if the entire attack-decay phase has not completed and the synthesizer is still transforming harmonics from attack to release. With very short attack and decay times this will not happen, but with long ones it will happen when playing and releasing notes quickly.

In addition, if the release time is set to 0, sound will cut off immediately when a note is released, which will almost always result in a pop or static sound. This may or may not be desirable. If it is not, keeping the release time at 10 milliseconds or so will prevent it.

LFO Settings

The low-frequency oscillator is an oscillator that can be used to add vibrato to sounds that are playing. The range is from 0 to 20 oscillations per second with a resolution of a tenth of a second. The vibrato waveform can be changed, and the modulation wheel will control the depth (intesity) of the vibrato.

The Keyboard

The keyboard is both a controller and indicator of notes playing. When a note is playing, its key will show a red dot. Notes can be triggered by clicking on them, by typing on the computer keyboard, or by using an external MIDI controller.

If playing via the mouse, notes will release as soon as the mouse button is released. in the "main" part of the window. However, if the mouse is released in the "harmonics" section of the synth -- which includes the harmonics editor panel and the peak and polyphony info section below it -- then notes will continue to play. You will need to do this if you want to keep notes playing while you tinker with the harmonics. Another interesting thing to note is that if you have multiple notes playing, right-clicking a lit note will deactivate it.

When playing notes with the keyboard, each row of keys will represent a different octave with the function keys F1 through F12 being the lowest octave and row of keys starting with Z being the highest.

CPU Usage

Additive synthesis is notorious for requiring a lot of CPU power.

Depending on synthesizer settings, each note that is playing can take as many as 35 million calculations per second. At a sample rate of 44100 samples per second and with 128 harmonic oscillators, SigmaTizm has to do a large amount of math, especially when you figure in calculating volume envelope and interpolation of harmonics from attack to sustain phase and the LFO. If you find your system slowing down and generating choppy sound, the two things that will reduce CPU usage the most will be playing fewer notes or using fewer harmonics in the sounds you have created.

Generating one of the 'canned' waveforms like square, triangle, or saw will result in the creation of either 64 or 128 harmonics. Since the highest harmonics will not normally be audible or will create aliasing sounds, it may be a good idea to erase them (right-click and drag in the harmonics editor) to save CPU cycles.

Other Controls

The "Bank" and "Patch" spinners are used for changing patches on an external MIDI device. They will only function if MIDI output is enabled.

The exclamation point icon is a "panic" button that turns all MIDI notes off.

The info button shows the about box.

If you're reading this you know what the question mark does.

The icon that looks like a MIDI connector lets you change MIDI settings.

The wrench icon lets you choose which sound device to use for output. SigmaTizm will use your primary audio device by default, but you can use any of your ASIO, DirectSound, or MME devices for output as long as they support standard CD-quality output.

The disk and folder icons let you save and load patches, respectively. This is so you can save sounds you have created for later recall. SigmaTizm comes with a few built-in patches as examples of sounds that can be created.

The two bars at the right are the peak level indicator. They let you know how loud the playing sounds are in relation to the maximum. If the peak indicators are more than about three-quarters full you will be getting some distortion from the synthesizer due to built in soft-limiting. This may or may not be desirable.

The first vertical slider at the left of the keyboard is the pitch wheel. It will change the pitch of the synthesizer by two semitones in either direction and also works for control of external MIDI devices.

The second vertical slider is the modulation wheel, which is used for mixing in vibrato from the LFO.
