Additive Synthesis Overview

An additive synthesizer works by adding together sine wave oscillators at different frequencies to generate sound.

The mathematician Joseph Fourier discovered that all periodic sounds could be created by adding together sine waves at different frequencies. For his theory he used an infinite number of sine waves, but since computers can't generate an infinite number of sine waves in real-time, it's usually safe to restrict those to audible frequencies.

Harmonic additive synthesis works by only using harmonics -- frequencies that are a multiple of the root, or fundamental tone being played. If you are playing an A note at 110 Hertz (cycles per second), then harmonics of that note will be at even multiples of that frequency -- 220Hz, 330Hz, 440Hz, 550Hz, and so on. By setting the loudness of each of these harmonics you can create sounds that have a certain timbre when all of the harmonics are added together. This is the method used by SigmaTizm and most other additive synthesizers.

In addition, it is typical for a timbre to change over time, with different tonal characteristics during the attack (when a string is plucked or a bell struck) versus when a note is being sustained (ringing or resonating). Most additive synthesizers will give you a way to transition from an attack sound of a certain timbre to a sustain sound of a different timbre. SigmaTizm lets you do this by specifying the attack and sustain harmonics separately via the harmonics editor window.
